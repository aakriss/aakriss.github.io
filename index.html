Rubik’s Cube Design Journey

The World’s Smallest Rubik’s Cube Attempt

This project started with the goal of building the smallest functional Rubik’s cube in the world—smaller than the existing 5.6mm record. I set my target at 5.0mm. I designed the cube from scratch in SolidWorks, reverse-engineering mechanisms from standard cubes but ultimately creating a completely custom system. The biggest challenge was how to connect the center pieces to the spherical core without screws or springs. I developed snap-fit prongs under 0.2mm wide—small enough to fit inside the cube yet strong enough to stay in place once assembled.

I researched the flexural modulus of various 3D printable materials and calculated the dimensions needed for the prongs to flex just enough to snap into place during assembly but remain rigid under regular rotation. Since the part dimensions were well under 1mm, I sought out micro-fabrication companies capable of printing with 10-50 micron resolution. Quotes ranged from $350 to $2500 for one unit, so I began testing larger versions with PLA on a standard FDM printer to validate the design.

The first test failed due to loose tolerances. I rebuilt the model with fully parametric spacing adjustments between all interlocking components. I discovered through iterative prints that zero spacing worked best, contrary to my expectations. I refined the design, adapted the prongs for a high-stiffness resin, and coordinated with a university lab to fabricate it. Unfortunately, Rubik’s released their own 5mm cube before my part could be printed, so I redesigned mine at 4.5mm but never fabricated it.

From Small to Fast: The Custom Speedcube

Pivoting from the record, I focused on performance. I designed a new cube optimized for speedcubing—combining features from several commercial cubes with my own innovations. This version used traditional hardware: springs, screws, and magnets. The geometry was again fully parametric so I could fine-tune dimensions across dozens of prints.

I tested different nozzle sizes and layer heights, settling on 0.2mm nozzle and 0.06mm layers for the final prototypes. Surprisingly, the rougher surface created by FDM printing helped retain cube lubricant, enhancing the tactile feel. However, support material posed a major issue, so I tested different orientations, support styles, and interface distances. I even printed full matrices of parts with systematically varied support parameters to find the best combination for finish and function.

Key Innovations and Features

This project involved innovations in mechanical design, fabrication strategy, and tolerance control. I created parametric CAD models allowing fast, repeatable iterations. I developed press-fit test blocks to gauge support-material interaction. I experimented with part splits and internal alignment tabs for support-free assembly. I also accounted for post-processing effects like resin shrinkage and PLA warping by including margin-of-error tolerances in critical interfaces.

One component of the final cube was a modern magnet system integrated into the mechanical architecture:

  • Edge-Corner Magnets: Inserted mid-print, they align layers automatically to reduce misalignment at high speeds.
  • Piece-Core Magnets: Provide mid-turn stability by pulling pieces toward the spherical core.
  • Maglev System: Replaces springs with opposing magnets to fine-tune cube tension and reduce friction.

These systems worked in tandem with optimized part spacing, lubricated interfaces, and a structure that could be tuned and rebuilt quickly. The final result was a high-performance, fully 3D-printed magnetic speedcube capable of competing with commercial flagship models.
